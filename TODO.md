### Standing rules

- **The trap that breaks a minor whenever it adds a column: the column must be nullable or defaulted.** A `NOT NULL` column with no default means the previous image's `INSERT`s fail against the new database, and by the mechanical test that quietly turns a minor into a **major**. It is the only way a minor accidentally violates its own versioning. `v1.6.0`'s two new tables (`credentials`, `push_subscriptions`), `v1.8.0`'s eight `applications` columns, and `v1.9.0`'s visa/hiring/timezone fields all shipped clean under it; the open `v1.11` batch adds no column at all, so the trap cannot fire here, but the rule stands for any future v1 minor. A column shipped loose under this rule is deferred, not forgotten: the `2.0.0` schema re-optimization item below owns the tightening pass, so the debt has an address.
- **Field admission test, for any new per-application column** (channel, agency, comp structure, language level, hiring entity, timezone, all of them): it must be **captured at prefill time by `UrlPrefillService`, or cost near-zero manual entry**. The app has one user, and a field he stops filling in after the fifth application is dead schema plus form friction. Nothing in the open plan adds a field, so this bites next on `2.0.0`'s `positions` entity and the tightening pass; it stays here as the standing test every future field is measured against.
- **Perishable-facts refresh rule, for every Japan-market item.** Each embeds perishable external facts: fee schedules, processing times, survey medians. A career tool that confidently states last year's rules is worse than one that says nothing, so each such item must state its **annual refresh cost** when it is scoped, and the sum of those lines is a real cap on how many of these a solo maintainer can ship. The open plan carries no market item (the visa/HSP research shipped with `v1.9.0`–`v1.10.0`); the rule governs the `2.0.0` career-intelligence item next, which already budgets this way ("one data-entry session a year").

## `2.0.0` and beyond: triggered by accepting an offer, not by finishing the `v1.11` batch

KarirKalyan's long-term identity is a **career growth tracker**, not just an application tracker: the name already says so (karir = career; the product is currently narrower than its own name). This whole cluster sits **outside the v1.x plan**, not because it is unimportant, but because `positions` is the `2.0.0` (it adds a table *and* changes what `accepted` means in the FSM), and its scoping trigger is a date in the user's life, not a release number. Giving it a release slot in the plan would contradict that.

- [ ] **`positions` (tenure) entity: the keystone; design it before building anything below.** *(`2.0.0`, the only major in this file)* Today `accepted` is a terminal status; a career tracker needs the job you then held as a first-class thing: company, title, start/end, and the comp structure `v1.8.0` shipped (normalized 年収). Comp history across positions (benchmarkable against the TokyoDev medians cited below), resume versioning, internal-promotion stages, and 職務経歴書 generation all hang off it: a 職務経歴書 is literally a positions table rendered as prose. Write it into SPEC.md's data model **first**; retrofitting it later means migrating what `accepted` means. **Scoping trigger: accepting an offer**, not finishing a prior release; the moment the search succeeds is the moment this entity is needed, or the app exits the user's life exactly when its retention story was supposed to begin.
- [ ] **Make the app survive its own success** *(post-`2.0.0`: every sub-item below hangs off `positions`)*. Today the lifecycle ends at `accepted`: the search closes and there is no reason to open the app again until the next one. Extend it into the tenure _between_ searches, so landing a job is a state change, not an exit:
  - **Resume as a living document**: the resume is already stored per application; add a current, versioned profile that keeps improving while employed, so the next application starts from the best version rather than a year-old PDF. Feeds the rirekisho/shokumu-keirekisho generation idea below.
  - **Low-noise market watch**: `wishlist` already models "interesting, not applied"; let it be the employed-mode default. Tracking relevant roles while happily employed is how you know your market value and when to move.
  - **Skills & certs against the market, not a wishlist**: `UrlPrefillService` already reads postings with Claude; extract the skills/certifications each tracked role asks for at prefill time, and the gap between what target roles want and what the profile holds becomes a ranked "learn next" list (JLPT level, cloud certs, a framework) with zero extra data entry. Goals can target the current employer too: an internal promotion has stages like an application does.
  - **Periodic check-ins**: Solid Queue and the mailer exist; a quarterly "update your resume, review your goals" nudge is one recurring job. Same dead-zone awareness as the calendar-aware follow-ups that shipped in `v1.4.0`.

  This repositions the tracker from a search tool you abandon on success into a career companion with a retention story: also the better portfolio argument, since every sub-item reuses machinery that already exists (bytea storage, `wishlist`, prefill extraction, recurring jobs) rather than demanding new infrastructure.

  **Scope boundary:** career growth does **not** mean drifting into a **networking CRM**, a **habit tracker**, or a **learning tracker**. Those are separate products with mature incumbents, and a broad-shallow app is a worse portfolio piece than a narrow-deep one. The admission test for any career-growth feature: **it must reuse the FSM, the timeline, the prefill extraction, or the `positions` entity**; if it needs none of them, it belongs in a different app. Concretely: contacts go no further than the recruiter-channel item's `agencies` table (that models submission ownership, not relationships); skills/certs/JLPT go no further than the gap list derived at prefill time (a ranked "learn next", never course progress or streaks); check-ins stay a recurring nudge, never a journaling habit loop.

- [ ] **Career intelligence: benchmark reports from published surveys** *(post-`2.0.0`: its headline view, comp percentile for the current position, needs `positions`. The offer-comp-vs-median slice became buildable when `v1.8.0` landed the 年収 structure, but the settled call is to keep it bundled here: no annual refresh-cost commitment before `positions` makes the full view buildable.)* An in-app, yearly-refreshed summary of reliable market sources: the [TokyoDev annual survey](https://www.tokyodev.com/articles/the-2025-tokyodev-developer-survey-results-are-live) first (2025: 989 respondents; median ¥9.5M, split by employer type, experience band, and language use), with MHLW's official wage statistics (賃金構造基本統計調査) as the government counterpart. The point is not the report (a static summary page would fail the career-growth admission test) but the user's own data shown against it: comp percentile for the current position (needs the `positions` entity), offer comp vs the market median for their experience band at decision time, their Japanese level against the language-requirement distribution of the roles they track. Implementation is a versioned reference dataset (a seed file refreshed once per survey cycle, no scraping, no pipeline), so the recurring cost is one data-entry session a year. Check each source's licensing/attribution terms before shipping its numbers.
- [ ] **Move the API off always-on Rails to serverless (Hono or similar) once the job search succeeds** *(post-job; the one infrastructure item in this cluster, riding the same life trigger, not a career-growth feature, so the admission test above does not apply)*. Today Rails is the right choice on purpose: the repo's job is Tokyo hiring signal, and the ~$6.30/month Railway bill (api ~$4.40 of it at a 0.44 GB average, measured 2026-07-20) is the price of an always-warm demo whose first hit is a screener clicking the link. The moment the job lands, that rationale expires and cost wins; same trigger-shape as the Cloudflare-proxy revisit. Target a genuinely free tier: AWS Lambda's permanent free tier or Cloudflare Workers, both of which Hono targets natively. **Railway itself has no free tier** (Hobby is a $5/month minimum), so "free on Railway" is not on the menu; staying there only shrinks the bill, it cannot zero it. Scope recorded now so the future pass starts honest: Solid Queue's persistent poller is the incompatible piece (the recurring jobs move to platform cron triggers), Postgres needs a home either way (serverless pairs it with connection pooling, or the data moves to a free-tier store), and cold starts stop mattering once nobody is being impressed by the demo. Versioning is not worth pre-deciding: by the mechanical test a rewrite that keeps the schema is not automatically a major; it sits in this cluster because the trigger is the same date, not because it is the `2.0.0`.
- [ ] **Settle the v1.x schema debt in the first major: re-optimize every table change the minor releases were forced to leave loose** *(the second infrastructure item in this cluster; rides whichever major actually ships, because a major is the only release where a backward-incompatible migration is free)*. The nullable-or-defaulted standing rule above is a deliberate trade, minor-release safety now for schema looseness later, and this item is where the bill gets settled. The pass, table by table over everything v1.x added (`credentials` and `push_subscriptions` from `v1.6.0`, the eight nullable `applications` columns from `v1.8.0`, and the visa/hiring/timezone fields from `v1.9.0`): backfill and tighten to `NOT NULL` or a real default wherever the domain actually requires the value, promote free-text columns to check-constrained enums where the value set has proven closed in practice, add the indexes deferred on growth grounds, and drop any column the field admission test killed in use, since a field the user stopped filling is dead schema and the major is the one chance to delete it cheaply. **The `timeline_entries` offer-lookup index is parked here outright** (it left the conditional list in the 2026-07-22 cleanup): `avg_days_to_offer` filters `to_status = 'offer'` with deliberately no index on `to_status`, and at personal-tracker scale a user's timeline is a few hundred rows already reachable through `(application_id, created_at)`; nothing in v1.x grows that table, so a major backfill pass is its only real home. Add `(to_status, application_id, created_at)` here if the table has grown by then, not before. One exception recorded now so the pass does not over-tighten: sponsorship stays nullable by design, because unknown is decision-relevant signal, not missing data (`v1.9.0`'s admission-test note is the record). If the serverless move above has happened by then, the tightening still applies unchanged: that rewrite keeps the schema, and a loose schema under a new runtime is the same debt wearing a different API.
- [ ] **Rirekisho + shokumu-keirekisho generation** *(deliberately parked past `2.0.0`; ship both together, after it)*. Japanese applications conventionally want two documents: 履歴書 (personal history, a standardised form) and 職務経歴書 (career history, free-form). Generating both as PDFs from stored profile data would be the clearest possible signal that the author understands the market. 履歴書 alone is a legal minor (additive, no schema break), but its twin *is* a `positions` table rendered as prose, and shipping half the pair, then maintaining a PDF toolchain for a year before the other half is even buildable, is the worst version of the trade. **Format question answered (2026-07-11):** the JIS template was indeed [withdrawn from the JIS standards in July 2020](https://japan-dev.com/blog/japanese-resume-rirekisho) (stationery stores still sell "JIS-compliant" pads, but it is a dead standard), and MHLW published a recommended replacement format (厚生労働省様式) designed for fair hiring: [gender optional; commute time, dependents, and spouse fields removed](https://www.gtalent.jp/blog/japanwork-en/job-hunting-en/rirekisho-en). **Target the MHLW format**: it is the closest thing to official, and its fair-hiring rationale is a good line in the README. **Carry cost (recorded 2026-07-11): the highest in this file.** PDF generation in Rails is a known maintenance sink (toolchain choice, CJK font embedding, layout drift), and the MHLW format itself can churn. Highest wow, highest carry; scope it with both eyes open, the way the dark-mode decision (`CHANGELOG.md` § Decisions) weighed its cost side. **Storage of the pair is parked with it:** typed document slots for uploading a 履歴書/職務経歴書 alongside the English resume were considered for v1.x and deferred: more blobs on the pg_dump-backed database (the same cost the no-version-history decision refused) for documents whose real payoff arrives when generation ships. If uploads are wanted sooner, the shape is a typed `documents` table reusing the `v1.4.4` filename machinery, not more bytea columns.
